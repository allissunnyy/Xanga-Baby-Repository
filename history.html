History

Impressionism revolutionized art by capturing light, color, and modern life.

Impressionism Timeline

Rejected by the rigid French Salon system, Impressionists organized their own independent exhibitions starting in 1874. They shocked critics with loose brushwork, bright colors, and everyday subjects—choosing Parisian cafés, train stations, and sunny gardens over classical mythology.

This radical shift from studio painting to plein air (outdoor) work captured the fleeting effects of sunlight and atmosphere. The movement's name came from Claude Monet's Impression, Sunrise, mockingly titled by a critic who called their work "unfinished sketches."

Future master of movement and modernist composition.

Claude Monet Born

The future "father of Impressionism" enters the world.

1841

Renoir & Morisot Born

Pierre-Auguste Renoir and Berthe Morisot (first female Impressionist) both born this year.

1844

Mary Cassatt Born

American Impressionist who brings the movement to the United States.

1863

Manet Shocks Paris

Olympia and Luncheon on the Grass rejected by Salon, inspire future Impressionists.

1870

Sisley: Canal Saint-Martin

Alfred Sisley paints quiet urban waterway, perfecting plein air technique.

1872

Monet: Impression, Sunrise

The painting that gives the movement its name. First Impressionist exhibition soon follows.

1874

First Impressionist Exhibition

Degas paints The Ballet Rehearsal. Morisot shows The Cradle.

1876

Renoir Peak: Moulin de la Galette

Celebration of Parisian joy. Degas paints Absinthe Drinker.

1881

Renoir: Luncheon of the Boating Party

Social harmony captured in glowing color.

1886

Cassatt: The Child's Bath

Renoir paints The Umbrellas. Morisot: In the Dining Room.

1890

Monet: Haystacks Series

Revolutionary study of light changing the same subject.

1897

Pissarro: Boulevard Montmartre

Urban Impressionism captures modern Paris.
